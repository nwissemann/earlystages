books

“Show me a family of readers, and I will show you the people who move the world.”

— Napoleon Bonaparte

And although this is the same man who (allegedly) wrote “I will return in three days; don’t wash.” to his wife Josephine, he still has a point.

Books are a way to access the life’s work of great (wo)men, condensed into a few hundred pages, for nothing more than a few euros.

Below you can find a list of the books that influenced me the most, or at least left the biggest impression.

If you end up reading any of them, let me know how you liked them!

  • a man’s search for meaning — victor frankl
    Even if all your freedoms are taken (like the author’s during the Shoah), you still have the freedom to choose how you react.
  • skin in the game — nassim nicholas taleb
    Many problems in the world stem from asymmetric risks: decision-makers benefit from upside while not suffering the downside.
  • hell yeah or no — derek sivers
    Say yes to risky growth opportunities. Still, if it’s not a “hell yeah”, it should probably be a “no.”
  • the defining decade — meg jay
    Contrary to popular belief, your twenties are not about drifting — they are about exploration, identity-building, and direction.
  • waking up — sam harris
    The self is not a singular entity. Look closely enough and it dissolves entirely — a core insight of duality and non-duality meditation.
  • the pathless path — paul millerd
    It’s easy to follow prestigious default careers. Harder — and more rewarding — to walk your own path.
  • die with zero — bill perkins
    Saving everything for later ignores a simple truth: youth is when experiences are most valuable.
  • beyond the wand — tom felton
    A surprisingly honest memoir about vulnerability, fame, and the strength it takes to open up.
  • irresistible — adam alter
    Technology addiction is an arms race. But with the right behavioral architecture, we can fight back.
  • dopamine nation — anna lembke
    The same neurotransmitter that motivates us can make us miserable — unless we learn how to work with it.
  • siddhartha — hermann hesse
    Some things can’t be taught. They must be lived.
  • leonardo da vinci — walter isaacson
    To understand the whole, observe the details first. Leonardo mastered this better than anyone.
