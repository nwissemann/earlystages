what if your 20s aren’t meant to be the best years of your life?

I recently had a convo with my boss about some life decisions, and she gave me great advice:

“Your 20s are for learning, not for maximizing income.”

I couldn’t agree more.

There are still so many years ahead to pay mortgages and make real money in some fancy job.

But our 20s are the best time to learn, explore, and take bold risks:

  • No children (usually)
  • Low living expenses and fewer financial responsibilities
  • High flexibility and social mobility
  • Fewer long-term obligations (like caring for a dog, a mortgage, or aging parents)

Now, having hit the halfway mark of my 20s, this sparked a bigger question:

What do I want to optimize for in this decade?

the best years of your life? or just the most challenging?

They say your 20s are supposed to be the best time of your life. Statistically, that’s questionable.

most decisions are large-world problems

Most life decisions in your 20s aren’t small-world problems like a game of chess — where you can know all the outcomes and calculate odds.

They’re large-world problems: ambiguous, high-stakes, and hard to predict.

  • What should I do with my life?
  • Is this the right career, city, relationship?
  • What if I make the wrong choice?

No one can tell you. There’s no simulation mode. There are too many unknowns.

no wonder so many people report their 20s as one of the least happy decades in life

Despite the social media hype, your 20s are often not the happiest time of your life. Studies show this decade is one of the most stressful:

  • Identity formation – pressure to “find your purpose” while dealing with uncertainty
  • Financial instability – low pay, student loans, rising cost of living
  • Social comparison – everyone seems to be marrying, launching startups, or getting dream jobs

Source: https://www.reddit.com/r/dataisbeautiful/comments/1alwlu9/whats_the_selfreported_depression_rate_by_age/

This is not to say you can’t make them the best time of your life. It’s merely to say that if you’ve been worrying about the expectation–experience gap: hold tight, the best time is (statistically) yet to come.

And there’s hope. These stressors can be growth triggers for building a life you love.

optimize for ‘identity capital’

In one of my favorite books, The Defining Decade, psychologist Meg Jay introduces a powerful idea: identity capital.

> Identity capital is our collection of personal assets. It is the repertoire of individual resources that we assemble over time.

> These are the investments we make in ourselves, the things we do well enough, or long enough, that they become a part of who we are.

What counts as identity capital:

  • Tangible: degrees, job experience, credentials, side projects
  • Intangible: confidence, clarity, networks, self-awareness, emotional skills

It’s how we build ourselves. It’s the currency of adulthood we use to get the jobs, relationships, and other things we want.

It’s a nudge to invest intentionally. That doesn’t mean having everything figured out. But it means doing things that build toward someone you want to be.

identity capital maximizes the amount of optional future paths

Let’s revisit Nassim Taleb’s concept of optionality.

At its core, optionality simply refers to having a wide range of choices available.

For example, if you build a skill that opens up many different career paths, you possess more optionality than someone whose skill limits them to only a few job options.

The key benefit of optionality is that in an unpredictable world, it allows you to succeed even without knowing exactly what the future holds.

> “Options, any options, by allowing you more upside than downside, are vectors of antifragility.

> – Nassim Taleb, Antifragility

In your 20s, you may not know what you want long-term. That’s why it’s smart to build options rather than fixed plans early in life:

  • Build transferable skills (problem-solving, writing, speaking, collaboration)
  • Try different projects, roles, industries, or geographies
  • Create things: a portfolio, a blog, a personal brand, a side hustle

Options create both leverage to negotiate and freedom in decision-making.

Optionality is not always the best strategy. If you’re aiming for a very specific path (e.g. malaria vaccine research), you do need to specialize early and give up alternatives.

Still, for most of us, early generalism and flexibility pay greater dividends than over-narrowing too soon.

your 20s don’t need to be optimized for money, stability, or perfection.

They’re a decade to build identity capital as it gives you the freedom to shape your life.

Forget the pressure of the “perfect plan.” Most choices you make now are reversible or reframable.

Explore widely. Try things out. Take detours. Invest in who you're becoming, not just what you're doing.
